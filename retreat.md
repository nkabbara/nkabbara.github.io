I had been meditating for over a decade. I started with Vipassana then a few year later switched to dzocheng. My practice consisted of sitting between 10 to 30 minutes daily, originally in the evenings, but then I switched to mornings when I realized that that is when my thoughts were the most scattered. Though my sessions were short, they were consistent with some weekend interruptions. If I don't practice for a few days in a row, my mood and sense of happiness is viserally felt.

Recently I realized that my instructions mainly derived from books and The Waking Up app, but that I had never thought in person instruction for something thad had become a fundemental part of my being. Looking around the Buenos Aires area, I found a vipassana center about an hour off town that offered a 10-day silent retreat with intensive practice of meditating for 10 hours a day. I signed up with the intention of deepening my practice, but walked out of there with much more.

It turned out what I had thought of as vipassana practice, which I had bee doing for years, was the pre training that enbled one to perform vipassana meditation.I had been doing step 0 for years and that alone was improving my life tremendously. Step 1 was training the mind to focus on the breath through the whole nose area. While doing so, maintain equanimity. Observe without aversion and without craving full of objectivity. Step 2 is training the mind to sense the subtleties that are manifesting in the nose area. Things like hot and cold air, electric sensations, pressure, numbness, and itching. Doing so while maintainig equanimity. Step 3 is reducing the area of focus to the area under the nose and obove the upper lip keeping awareness and equanimity as your guide. These first few steps are used to train your mind to learn how to focus, to train in equanimity, and expand the awareness of your senses. At step 4 is where vipassan starts.

Vipassana is a collection of techniques that are meant to teach you how to communicate with your subconcious. Using the foundational practice, one technique is to pick a point at the top of your head and work your way down to the toes of your feet scanning each individual body part separately: back of head area, face area, neck, right arm and shoulder, righ upper arm including elbow, right forearm to wrist, right hand. Same on the left limb. Continue down to front of torso, then back of torso, pelvis, right leg in parts, left leg in parts. You could then repeat in reverse or start over. Do not use your imagination to scan, rather, use sensations. This continues to be a challenge for me because my mind tends to imagine the body parts rather than sense them. As you're doing this, you'll notice three types of sensations, a vibrating one, a contracted one, or a mix of the two in different body parts. With the vibrating ones, your scan continues freely, while with the contracted one, you might have to stay with them for up to a minute so they loosen into a vibrating sensation. If it doesn't you may continue and when you come back to it in a later scan, you may find it to be very different.Anothe variation of this technique is doing quick scans just following the freeflowing parts for a couple of times back and forth or only one way, then doing a part by part scan and staying with the contracted sensations.

For a non practicionar this all sounds really strange if not useless. But as you apply these techniques, you are teaching your mind to sense what your subconcious is up to through bodily sensations before they manifest into action, like speech or movements. You become more aware of your internal state before that could be a precursor to some automatic pattern that you harbor giving you a chance to alter behavior.

If you have a very stubborn contracted sensation, you may do a scan always starting from the top of your head and when you reach it, overlay a grid over it, and start to compare every square in the grid with the other in how they differ in sensation. You could then do that sideways and front to back. Then continue going down the body to finith the scan. 

In some instances, you may have complete free flow. In those cases, you may go deeper below your skin into your body sensing anything that could be happening inside. Be sure to not try to imagine organs. Keep sensation as the source of movement. When done with that, you may end the scan by sensing you spinal chord from top to bottom.

In all these thechniques, always start top to bottom, stay equanamous and aware of minute subtleties. End your sitting session with a few minutes of meta.

After learning all this and experiencing its power first hand, I have decided to follow the teacher's recommentation of meditating two hours a day, one in the morning before breakfast and one in the afternoon before nightfall.
